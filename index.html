LAMS

Under Review at NeurIPS 2024

Anonymous Authors

Abstract

Operating robotic manipulators through interfaces like joysticks often requires frequent switching between control modes, where each mode maps the joystick's movements to specific actions on the robot. This frequent switching can make teleoperation cumbersome and inefficient, especially in assistive applications where users with motor impairments rely on simplified control interfaces. Existing shared autonomy solutions, such as heuristic-driven or demonstration-based methods, tend to be task-specific and lack generalizability. In this paper, we introduce LLM-Driven Automatic Mode Switching (LAMS), which leverages Large Language Models (LLMs) to automatically switch control modes based on task context, enabling more intuitive control without requiring task-specific adjustments. Unlike existing approaches, LAMS requires no prior task demonstrations and incrementally adapts to user behavior over time by incorporating mode-switching examples into its prompts. We validate LAMS on long-horizon tasks through an ablation study and a user study with 10 participants, showing that LAMS significantly reduces manual switches and is preferred over other methods.

Out-of-Distribution Classification


This benchmark is used for practical out-of-distribution applications for learning-based EMG control interfaces. We present two types of out-of-distribution tasks for machine learning: A) intersubject classification, and B) adaptation using train-test splits for time-series.

Distribution shifts


Distribution shifts occur over multiple sessions and samples, and the model should generalize or adapt to these changes. This benchmark evaluates the model's ability to generalize or adapt to distribution shifts in the data.
